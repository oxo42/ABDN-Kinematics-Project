This project presents the kinematic modelling and MATLAB-based analysis of the
Dobot Magician Lite (DM-Lite) robot arm with a MATLAB implementation of the model.

\section{Introduction}

Robot manipulators are essential in industrial, medical and educational
domains because of their precision, repeatability and programmability
\cite{siciliano2009, craig2005}. Common applications include pick-and-place,
assembly, and laboratory automation. In educational environments, 
arms such as the DM-Lite are used for teaching robotics.

Classified as an anthropomorphic manipulator \cite{siciliano2009}, its all-revolute 
configuration offers superior dexterity and a large spherical workspace relative to its footprint. 
however, it lacks direct correspondence between joint DOFs and Cartesian space, resulting in variable 
positioning accuracy and significant gravitational stress compared to Cartesian or SCARA systems. 

This project develops a MATLAB-based model to evaluate its kinematics, Jacobian, singularities, and dynamic behavior.

\section{Kinematic Modelling}
\label{sec:kinematic_modelling}

\subsection{Robot configuration and assumptions}
The kinematic model treats the robot as a 4R serial manipulator, with
$\theta_1$ describing base rotation, $\theta_2$ and $\theta_3$ defining planar
arm motion, and $\theta_4$ controlling tool orientation.

\subsubsection{The Passive Joint}
\label{subsec:passive_joint}

The physical robot is a series of 4-bar linkages \cite{cmuSimulation2022}. 

The passive joint P is introduced to maintain the constraint that the
end-effector remains parallel to the ground. This is achieved by defining the
passive joint angle as $-\theta_3-\theta_2$.

The final rotation into the end effector frame $\alpha_3$
happens at the end of the passive link, so the passive link's rotation still
applies to the frame from joint 3 as shown in Table~\ref{tab:dh_parameters}.

\subsection{Forward kinematics}

The forward kinematics of the DM-Lite were derived using the
Denavit-Hartenberg convention. Homogeneous transformation matrices were derived
for each joint and multiplied to create the transformation matrix from the base
frame to the end-effector frame, providing both rotation and cartesian position
\cite{craig2005, denavit1955}.

Let $r$ be the radial distance from the base $z$-axis to the wrist center:
\begin{equation}
    r = L_2 \cos(\theta_2) + L_3 \cos(\theta_2 + \theta_3)
\end{equation}
The Cartesian position of the end-effector is then:
\begin{align}
    x &= r \cos(\theta_1) \\
    y &= r \sin(\theta_1) \\
    z &= L_1 + L_2 \sin(\theta_2) + L_3 \sin(\theta_2 + \theta_3)
\end{align}


\subsection{Jacobian and singularity analysis}
The Jacobian matrix relates joint rates to end-effector motion. In this work,
the geometric Jacobian was used to describe the relationship between joint
velocities and the linear and angular velocity of the end-effector, while an
analytical form was considered for singularity analysis in terms of the selected
task variables \cite{siciliano2009, spong2006}. Jacobian-based analysis is
particularly useful for identifying configurations in which the manipulator
loses mobility or becomes difficult to control.

\subsubsection*{Geometric Jacobian}
The geometric Jacobian relates the joint velocities to the linear and angular
velocity of the end effector:
\[ \begin{bmatrix} v \\ \omega \end{bmatrix} = J_g(q) \dot{q} \]

The geometric Jacobian is therefore:
\[
J_g(q) = \begin{bmatrix}
-r \sin(\theta_1) & \cos(\theta_1)(-L_2 \sin(\theta_2) - L_3 \sin(\theta_2 + \theta_3)) & -L_3 \sin(\theta_2 + \theta_3) \cos(\theta_1) & 0 \\
r \cos(\theta_1) & \sin(\theta_1)(-L_2 \sin(\theta_2) - L_3 \sin(\theta_2 + \theta_3)) & -L_3 \sin(\theta_2 + \theta_3) \sin(\theta_1) & 0 \\
0 & L_2 \cos(\theta_2) + L_3 \cos(\theta_2 + \theta_3) & L_3 \cos(\theta_2 + \theta_3) & 0 \\
0 & -\sin(\theta_1) & -\sin(\theta_1) & 0 \\
0 & \cos(\theta_1) & \cos(\theta_1) & 0 \\
1 & 0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\]

\subsubsection*{Singularities}
A singularity occurs when the determinant of the analytical Jacobian becomes zero:
\[ \det(J) = L_2 L_3 \sin(\theta_3) r = 0 \]
This expression shows that singularities occur when either $\sin(\theta_3) = 0$ or $r = 0$. The condition $\sin(\theta_3) = 0$ corresponds to the arm being fully stretched or fully folded, so that the links become collinear. The condition $r = 0$ corresponds to the wrist lying on the base axis, where the manipulator loses motion authority in certain horizontal directions. Near these configurations, the velocity mapping becomes ill-conditioned and large joint velocities may be required to generate small end-effector motions \cite{siciliano2009, spong2006}.

\subsection{Inverse kinematics}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \input{figures/inverse_xy_plane.tex}
    \caption{Top-view geometry showing the base joint angle $\theta_1$ relative to the Cartesian coordinates $(x, y)$.}
\end{figure}

The base joint angle is:
\[ \theta_1 = \operatorname{atan2}(y, x) \]

The remaining problem is reduced to a planar two-link geometry in the $r$--$z$
plane \cite{craig2005}. Define $z' = z - L_1$ as the height relative to the
shoulder. The function $\operatorname{atan2}(y,x)$ is used because
it returns the correct angular quadrant from the Cartesian coordinates
\cite{mathworks2023atan2}. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \input{figures/inverse_rz_plane.tex}
    \caption{$r$--$z$ plane geometry used for inverse kinematics of the shoulder and elbow joints.}
\end{figure}

Two solution branches exist for reachable targets: an elbow-up configuration and
an elbow-down configuration. Using the law of cosines in the triangle formed by
$L_2, L_3$, and the distance to the target $h = \sqrt{r^2 + (z')^2}$:
\begin{equation}
    \cos \beta = \frac{L_2^2 + h^2 - L_3^2}{2 L_2 h}
\end{equation}
The shoulder angles are then:
\[ \theta_{2u} = \phi + \beta \quad \text{and} \quad \theta_{2d} = \phi - \beta \]
where $\phi = \operatorname{atan2}(z', r)$ is the angle from the horizontal to the target.

For $\theta_3$, the forearm angle, we have two cases as well: \textbf{elbow-up} and \textbf{elbow-down}. However, the derivation for the inverse kinematic equation for this angle is a bit more complex.

Given
\begin{align}
    r^2 = x^2 + y^2 \\
    z' = z - L_1 \\
    r^2 = L_2 cos(\theta_2) + L_3 cos(\theta_2 + \theta_3) \\
    z' = L_2 sin(\theta_2) + L_3 sin(\theta_2 + \theta_3)
\end{align}

Adding $r^2$ and $z'^2$ and simplifying gives us

\begin{align}
    \cos \theta_3 = \frac{r^2 + z'^2 - L_2^2 - L_3^2}{2L_2L_3} \\
    \sin \theta_3 = \sqrt{1 - \cos^2 \theta_3}
\end{align}

And the value of $\sin \theta_3$ above is negative for the elbow-up condition
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{3d} = \operatorname{atan2}(\sin \theta_3, \cos \theta_3) \quad \text{(for elbow-down)}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{3u} = \operatorname{atan2}(-\sin \theta_3, \cos \theta_3) \quad \text{(for elbow-up)}
\end{equation}

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Kinematic Modelling Approach}

The kinematic model of the DM-Lite was developed using the Denavit--Hartenberg (DH) convention. Each joint was
modelled as a revolute joint, and the robot was approximated as an open
kinematic chain, despite the physical presence of a four-bar linkage mechanism.

\subsection{Inverse Kinematics Strategy}
A geometric approach was selected for the inverse kinematics problem, leveraging 
the direct geometric relationships between joint angles and end-effector positions. 

This approach was preferred over numerical methods due to its computational
efficiency, analytical clarity, and suitability for real-time
applications\cite{mathworks2023, craig2005}.

\subsection{Dynamic Modelling Approach}
The dynamic behaviour of the manipulator was considered using the standard
rigid-body robot dynamics formulation \cite{siciliano2009, spong2006}
\[ \tau = M(q)\ddot{q} + C(q, \dot{q})\dot{q} + G(q) \]
where $\tau$ is the vector of joint torques, $M(q)$ is the inertia matrix, $C(q,
\dot{q})\dot{q}$ represents Coriolis and centrifugal effects, and $G(q)$ is the
gravity vector.

In the present project, the dynamic equations were incorporated at a conceptual
and modelling level to extend the kinematic model developed in MATLAB. However,
a complete high-fidelity dynamic implementation was limited by the lack of
detailed physical parameters such as link masses, mass centres, and inertia
tensors from the manufacturer documentation \cite{siciliano2009}. Therefore, the
work focused on establishing the dynamic form of the equations and explaining
how such terms would be integrated into the MATLAB model for future extension.

\subsection{MATLAB Implementation}

The robot model was implemented as described in
Section~\ref{sec:kinematic_modelling} as a MATLAB class with an API allowing for
moving the joint angles, setting the end effect location in 3D space and viewing
the transformation matrices, jacobian etc. See dobot.m \cite{githubDobot} for the full implementation.

A UI \cite{githubUIRobot} was built (see Figure~\ref{fig:ui_robot}) to allow
users to interact with the model and unit tests (dobot\_tests.m
\cite{githubDobotTests}) were added to ensure the model stayed accurate over
time. A separate supplementary document, Mathematical Derivations.docx, is
included in the submission ZIP and contains the full step-by-step derivations
that were omitted from the main report for brevity.

\subsection{AI}
AI tools, specifically Gemini \& ChatGPT, were used to assist in generating MATLAB code for
the UI and unit tests. The dobot.m \cite{githubDobot} MATLAB class was written by hand as that
contains the mathematical model.

\section{Results and Validation}
The MATLAB model was validated through forward kinematics (FK), inverse kinematics (IK), and 
singularity testing. FK was verified by comparing computed end-effector positions against geometric expectations, 
while IK was validated by reconstructing joint configurations from target Cartesian coordinates. 
Consistency between these mappings confirms the model's internal mathematical integrity. 

Furthermore, tests for unreachable targets and singularity detection [6] demonstrate that the implementation correctly 
handles workspace edge cases. The results show strong agreement between the analytical derivations and the MATLAB implementation.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}

This project developed and validated a kinematic model of the DM-Lite using the Denavit–Hartenberg formulation. 
Forward and inverse kinematics, the Jacobian for differential motion, and singular configurations were derived analytically and 
implemented in MATLAB. The resulting model successfully reproduced valid end-effector positions while rejecting targets outside the 
reachable workspace.

A primary challenge was the robot's 4-bar linkage system; simplifying this to a serial chain initially caused the 
end-effector to lose its ground-parallel orientation, a problem addressed in Subsection 2.1.1. 

Another issue with the Inverse kinematics was solved by reliably computing solutions 
reducing the 3D problem to a planar geometric construction in the $r$–$z$ plane. Due to limited manufacturer data, link masses, centers of 
mass, and inertia tensors were estimated. 

While the current MATLAB UI utilizes a cosine easing function for joint-space animation, future work 
will employ the Jacobian to enable Cartesian path-following.
